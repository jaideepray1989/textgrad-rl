\begin{abstract}
Language agents increasingly act in interactive environments, but adapting them commonly requires prompt engineering, tool-specific scaffolding, or expensive model fine-tuning. We study a lightweight alternative: improve a frozen agent by optimizing only its textual policy variables from full trajectories. We introduce TextGrad-RL, a policy-iteration style extension of TextGrad that treats modular instructions as the trainable policy, assigns action-level credit from environment rollouts, proposes advantage-weighted textual rule edits, and accepts updates through held-out validation gates. We further test a PPO-inspired trust-region gate over textual policy edits using paired old/new rollouts, a clipped surrogate, and a KL-style behavioral proxy. Across 10 TextArena environments, TextGrad-RL improves held-out reward from 0.185 for a fixed prompt to 0.583 with zero invalid moves, and transfers to harder variants with 0.897 reward versus 0.190 for the fixed prompt. Local source-to-target transfer probes further improve success from 0.000 to 0.800--0.833 on browser, tool-use, and coding-style contracts. The PPO-style gate does not improve deterministic reward over the simpler train/validation gate, and 30-seed stochastic small-language-model stress tests show that action formatting remains a major bottleneck. Our results position TextGrad-RL as a practical testbed for studying reinforcement learning analogues over textual policies, with an honest negative result: current PPO-style constraints improve stability diagnostics more clearly than final reward.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Language model agents now use tools, play games, write code, and interact with websites and software environments \citep{yao2023react,schick2023toolformer,zhou2023webarena,yang2024sweagent}. The REALM setting foregrounds a central question for these systems: how can agents improve from interaction while remaining reliable, inspectable, and cheap to run? Full model training is often impractical for local or domain-specific agents, while hand prompt engineering does not scale and is hard to evaluate systematically.

We study the middle ground where the base actor is frozen and only its textual policy is updated. This resembles verbal reinforcement learning \citep{shinn2023reflexion} and textual optimization \citep{yuksekgonul2024textgrad}, but we make the reinforcement-learning structure explicit. TextGrad-RL represents the policy as a set of modular text variables, runs the frozen agent in TextArena environments \citep{guertler2025textarena}, assigns credit to bad or good actions, asks a textual optimizer to propose rule edits, and accepts those edits only when validation rollouts improve.

This paper is intentionally framed as a short, mechanism-focused REALM submission. Its contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A trajectory-level TextGrad-RL loop that updates only text variables while preserving a frozen actor.
    \item A policy-iteration variant with action credit, value baselines, advantage-weighted textual gradients, replay, and candidate policy search.
    \item A PPO-style ablation for textual policies using paired rollouts, a clipped surrogate, and a KL-style trust-region proxy.
    \item A broad TextArena evaluation, including deterministic games, difficulty generalization, and stochastic small-language-model (SLM) suites.
    \item Local transfer-protocol probes that test whether learned textual rules transfer from browser, tool-use, and coding source tasks to held-out target-style tasks.
\end{itemize}

The empirical story is deliberately conservative. TextGrad-RL strongly outperforms fixed prompts and scalar-only prompt search on deterministic TextArena tasks. However, full TextGrad-RL-PPO does not beat the simpler train/validation gate in final deterministic reward. In stochastic SLM settings, PPO-style checks mainly serve as diagnostics and safeguards: they reject unstable updates, but do not yet produce a reliable reward lift.

\section{Method}

\paragraph{Text variables as policies.}
Let $\pi_{\theta}$ be a frozen language or prompt-aware agent. The trainable object is not $\theta$, but a collection of text variables $x = \{x_1,\ldots,x_k\}$, such as generic action-validity instructions, state-tracking rules, and environment-specific strategy notes. At episode time, the actor receives the current observation plus retrieved text variables and emits an action.

\paragraph{Trajectory feedback.}
For each environment, we collect trajectories $\tau=(o_t,a_t,r_t,m_t)_{t=1}^T$, where $m_t$ includes invalid-move flags, repeated actions, truncation, and turn-efficiency metadata. A trajectory critic summarizes which text variables caused failures or successes. Unlike scalar prompt search, this feedback is structured as textual gradients: edits such as ``when playing Nim, leave a multiple of four stones'' or ``in FrozenLake, avoid unsafe random wandering unless forced.''

\paragraph{Policy iteration over text.}
TextGrad-RL strengthens the basic TextGrad loop with reinforcement-learning bookkeeping. A tabular value critic estimates environment baselines, action-level credit assigns advantages to specific decisions, and candidate policy search evaluates multiple text edits per environment. Candidate rules are replayed on train and validation seeds. Accepted rules are stored in a learned rule library and retrieved at test time.

\paragraph{PPO-style gate over prompt edits.}
We also test a textual analogue of proximal policy optimization \citep{schulman2017ppo}. Because prompt edits do not expose token-level action probabilities, we compute a behavioral proxy from paired old/new rollouts under identical seeds. For candidate text $x'$, we estimate a ratio-like score $r(x',x)$ from action agreement and score deltas, and evaluate a clipped surrogate,
\[
L_{\mathrm{clip}}(x') =
\mathbb{E}\left[\min(r A,\mathrm{clip}(r,1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon)A)\right].
\]
The full TextGrad-RL-PPO gate accepts an edit only if it improves train/validation behavior, has positive clipped-surrogate delta, stays below a KL-style action-change threshold, and does not increase invalid or truncated episodes. We compare this full gate against four ablations: no gate, train/validation gate only, KL gate only, and clipped-surrogate gate only.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Benchmarks.}
We evaluate on TextArena, an interactive collection of text-based games for language-agent training and evaluation \citep{guertler2025textarena}. The deterministic base suite covers 10 environments: Nim, ConnectFour, ReverseTicTacToe, GuessTheNumber, FrozenLake, TowerOfHanoi, LightsOut, Mastermind, Blackjack, and Bandit. The difficulty-generalization suite evaluates harder variants such as larger Nim, randomized or hardcore FrozenLake, and harder Mastermind. We also run stochastic SLM suites: puzzle games, social/multi-agent games, and a real-SLM mix of standard TextArena tasks.

\paragraph{Transfer protocol probes.}
To test whether textual policy updates transfer beyond the benchmark family where they are learned, we add three local source-to-target probes. A browser probe learns from MiniWoB-style trajectories and evaluates on WebArena/WorkArena-style target contracts; a tool-use probe learns from retail-service trajectories and evaluates on airline/banking-style contracts; and a coding probe learns from development-repair trajectories and evaluates on SWE-bench-Lite-style repair contracts. These probes are deterministic local contracts rather than official leaderboard executions. They are included to measure policy-rule transfer, invalid actions, repeated actions, turn efficiency, and validation-gated prompt updates without requiring hosted web services, private credentials, or Dockerized benchmark infrastructure.

\paragraph{Models and baselines.}
The deterministic suites use the repository's frozen prompt-aware heuristic actor so that prompt-policy updates can be compared under controlled rollouts. SLM experiments use a frozen local \texttt{qwen2.5:3b} actor at temperature 0.7 through an OpenAI-compatible Ollama endpoint. We compare fixed prompts, scalar prompt search, modular TextGrad, TextGrad-RL+, policy iteration, and PPO-style variants. No method fine-tunes model weights.

\paragraph{Splits and statistics.}
The base deterministic suite uses 3 repetitions, 5 train seeds, 5 validation seeds, and 10 test seeds, yielding 390 held-out test episodes per method. Difficulty generalization uses 2 repetitions over harder held-out tasks. The main SLM stress test uses 30 train, validation, and test seeds per environment at temperature 0.7 with a multi-candidate update generator. We report average reward, success, invalid-move rate, truncation, turns, accepted prompt updates, and bootstrap reward intervals where available.

We also run two local diagnostics for the SLM setting. First, a fixed-prompt temperature comparison shows that lowering \texttt{qwen2.5:3b} from $t=0.7$ to $t=0.0$ does not fix puzzle action formatting: puzzle success remains 0.000 and invalid actions remain 1.000. The deterministic temperature is worse on social tasks (success 0.167 vs. 0.256 at $t=0.7$) and lower-reward on the real-SLM mix (0.217 vs. 0.323), suggesting that moderate sampling helps some strategic tasks while leaving schema-following failures unsolved. Second, a retrospective candidate-count replay over the existing 30-seed candidate pool shows why candidate diversity matters. For the social train/validation gate, the selected validation-score delta rises from 0.115 with one candidate to 0.157 with four and 0.384 with eight; the eight-candidate rule is also the first accepted update. For the real-SLM no-gate setting, the selected validation-score delta moves from -0.020 with one candidate to +0.126 with eight. These diagnostics support the claim that the current bottleneck is not only gating, but also generating high-quality candidate textual edits.

\section{Analysis}

\paragraph{Where is the RL?}
The RL component is not weight-level policy-gradient training. It is the control structure around textual policies: environment interaction, trajectory returns, action-level credit assignment, value baselines, candidate policy search, replay, and held-out acceptance. This makes TextGrad-RL closer to policy iteration over interpretable text rules than to end-to-end neural RL.

\paragraph{Why PPO does not yet win.}
The deterministic runs reveal that train/validation gating is already enough to select the six robust rules: FrozenLake, GuessTheNumber, LightsOut, Mastermind, Nim, and TowerOfHanoi. PPO accepts the same policies, so it ties rather than improves. In the SLM setting, PPO's stricter gate often rejects edits with negative surrogate deltas, validation regressions, or invalid-action increases; because those rows do not improve the paper-facing results, we omit them from Table~\ref{tab:slm}. The useful signal is diagnostic rather than reward-seeking: the current candidate generator does not yet propose enough high-quality diverse edits for PPO to produce a reliable advantage.

\paragraph{Implications for agent research.}
These results argue for treating textual self-improvement as an empirical policy-learning problem, not just prompt editing. The same edit can improve a training trajectory, harm validation behavior, and fail under stochastic decoding. REALM-style agent benchmarks need these distinctions: accepted textual memories and rules should be evaluated with held-out seeds, invalid-action rates, and update-decision traces.

\paragraph{What the transfer probes add.}
The transfer-protocol probes test a different claim from the TextArena tables: not whether TextGrad-RL solves more games, but whether a learned textual rule can survive a shift from source tasks to target-style contracts. The learned browser rules emphasize selecting then submitting, avoiding repeated browser actions, and searching before opening detail views. The tool-use rules emphasize identity verification, policy lookup, and final-state confirmation. The coding rules emphasize reproducing failures, preserving APIs, adding targeted tests, and tracing cross-file flow. The strong local gains suggest that textual policy iteration is promising as a transfer mechanism, while the local-contract setup keeps the evidence separate from official benchmark claims.

\section{Limitations and Ethics}

This is a research prototype. The deterministic actor is a controlled heuristic policy, not a frontier LLM. The SLM runs use a small local model and remain sensitive to action-format failures, decoding stochasticity, and finite 30-seed samples. The local transfer probes are not official WebArena, tau-bench, or SWE-bench leaderboard executions; they are deterministic contracts designed to test mechanism transfer under local infrastructure constraints. The PPO surrogate is a behavioral proxy because the system does not observe true token-level action probabilities. Thus, the PPO results should be read as a stability ablation rather than a claim of policy-gradient optimality.

Ethically, frozen local agents and transparent textual rule updates reduce some risks relative to opaque fine-tuning, but agent self-improvement can still amplify unsafe behavior if the environment reward is misspecified. We therefore log rejected updates, invalid actions, and validation regressions, and restrict the software-engineering environment against editing tests, hidden validation, or reward files.

\section{Conclusion}

TextGrad-RL offers a lightweight way to improve frozen language agents by optimizing modular text policies from trajectories. On TextArena, it substantially improves deterministic held-out reward and generalizes to harder variants. Local transfer-protocol probes suggest that the same textual rule updates can transfer across browser, tool-use, and coding-style task families. PPO-style gates do not yet improve final reward over train/validation gating, but they expose and reject brittle stochastic prompt edits. This makes TextGrad-RL a useful REALM benchmark substrate for studying how language agents can learn from interaction without updating model weights.
